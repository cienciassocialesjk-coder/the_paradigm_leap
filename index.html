A game about how we learn

The Paradigm
Leap

Three steps, three ways of understanding learning. You'll build a student's cognitive scaffolding, guide them without handing over the answer, and design a real experience that changes their community. Each level is a different theory — and a different way of thinking like an educator.

How to play

  1. Level 1 — Constructivism: drag (or tap, then tap) each student behavior onto the Piaget cognitive stage it belongs to.
  2. Level 2 — Discovery learning: a student is stuck on a math concept. Choose clues, not answers, in the order that best leads them to deduce the solution themselves.
  3. Level 3 — Experiential: the school board just cut the arts budget. Decide, step by step, how to turn the problem into a hands-on, interdisciplinary project.
  4. No hard penalties: if you get it wrong, you get a hint and try again. XP only goes up.
Level 1 · Constructivism

The cognitive scaffolding

Piaget and Vygotsky understood development as an active construction, not as information being poured in. Drag each classroom observation onto the Piaget cognitive stage it describes. If a piece doesn't fit, no problem — just try again.

Level 2 · Discovery learning

The mystery of the missing fraction

Bruner argued that we understand best what we discover for ourselves. Jamie can't quite compare fractions. Don't give them the answer: choose the clue that, right now, gets them closer to figuring it out on their own.

Jamie "I don't get it... is 1/3 bigger than 1/4? Four is bigger than three, right? So 1/4 should win."

Level 3 · Experiential learning

The arts budget

Dewey championed "learning by doing": knowledge gets built by solving real problems. The school board just cut the Arts program's budget. Design, decision by decision, a response that turns this into a genuine educational experience for your students.

The principal announces at the staff meeting: "Unfortunately, the Arts program is losing 60% of its budget this semester. We need an alternative before March." You have a classroom of 28 high school students and the option to ask other departments and the community for support.
